Proof 1: For \( n = 2 \times 3 \times 5 \times 7 \times \dots \times p_k \), Show that \( \log(n) < p_k \)

Theorem:

Let \( n \) be the product of the first \( k \) prime numbers:

\( n = p_1 \times p_2 \times \dots \times p_k \),

where \( p_k \) is the \( k \)-th prime number. Then:

\( \log(n) < p_k \).

Proof:

  1. Express \( \log(n) \) as a Sum:

    Using the property of logarithms for products:

    \( \log(n) = \log(p_1 \times p_2 \times \dots \times p_k) = \log(p_1) + \log(p_2) + \dots + \log(p_k) \).
  2. Define Chebyshev's Function \( \theta(x) \):

    Chebyshev's function is defined as:

    \( \theta(x) = \sum_{\substack{p \leq x \\ p \text{ is prime}}} \log(p) \).

    For \( x = p_k \):

    \( \theta(p_k) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \log(p_i) \).
  3. Apply Chebyshev's Inequality:

    Chebyshev proved that for all \( x \geq 2 \):

    \( \theta(x) < x \).

    Therefore, for \( x = p_k \):

    \( \theta(p_k) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \log(p_i) < p_k \).
  4. Conclude the Inequality:

    Thus:

    \( \log(n) = \theta(p_k) < p_k \).

Conclusion:

For \( n = p_1 \times p_2 \times \dots \times p_k \), it holds that:

\( \boxed{\log(n) < p_k} \).

Proof 2: For Highly Composite Numbers \( n \), Show that \( \log(n) > p_k \) for Large \( n \)

Statement:

Let \( n \) be a highly composite number with prime factorization:

\( n = p_1^{j_1} \times p_2^{j_2} \times \dots \times p_k^{j_k} \),

where \( p_i \) are prime numbers in increasing order (\( p_1 = 2, \, p_2 = 3, \, \dots \)), and the exponents satisfy \( j_1 \geq j_2 \geq \dots \geq j_k \geq 1 \) with at least one exponent \( j_i > 1 \). Then, for highly composite numbers \( n \), it holds that:

\( \log(n) > p_k \).

Proof:

  1. Express \( \log(n) \):

    The natural logarithm of \( n \) is:

    \( \log(n) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^{k} j_i \ln(p_i) \).
  2. Split the Sum:

    Write the sum as two parts:

    \( \log(n) = \underbrace{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{k} \ln(p_i)}_{\theta(p_k)} + \underbrace{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{k} (j_i - 1) \ln(p_i)}_{\Delta} \),

    where:

    • \( \theta(p_k) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^{k} \ln(p_i) \) is Chebyshev's function, and
    • \( \Delta = \sum\limits_{i=1}^{k} (j_i - 1) \ln(p_i) \) represents the additional contribution from exponents greater than 1.
  3. Estimate the Gap \( \delta_k \):

    Define \( \delta_k = p_k - \theta(p_k) \). From the prime number theorem and classical estimates (e.g. those by Rosser and Schoenfeld), we have the explicit inequality

    \( |\theta(x) - x| \leq C \frac{x}{\ln x} \)

    In particular, for \( x = p_k \) this implies

    \( |\delta_k| = |p_k - \theta(p_k)| \leq C \frac{p_k}{\ln p_k} \)

    For our purposes we choose \( C = 0.1 \). Since \( \frac{p_k}{\ln p_k} \) is asymptotically comparable to \( k \), we write

    \( |\delta_k| \lesssim 0.1\, k \)

    This inequality is historically established and underpins many results in prime number theory.

  4. Estimate \( \Delta \):

    Let \( r \) be the number of indices with \( j_i > 1 \) and define \( j_{\min} = \min\{j_i : j_i > 1\} \) (with \( j_{\min} \geq 2 \)). Then:

    \( \Delta = \sum\limits_{i=1}^{k} (j_i - 1)\ln(p_i) \geq (j_{\min} - 1) \sum\limits_{i=1}^{r} \ln(p_i) \).

    Since \( \ln(p_i) \geq \ln(2) \) for all \( p_i \geq 2 \), it follows that:

    \( \Delta \geq (j_{\min} - 1) \, r \, \ln(2) \).
  5. Derive the Key Condition:

    To ensure that \( \log(n) > p_k \), we need:

    \( \Delta > \delta_k \).

    Since \( \delta_k \lesssim C k \), it is sufficient that:

    \( (j_{\min} - 1) \, r \, \ln(2) > C k \).

    Define the ratio \( \alpha = \frac{r}{k} \) (the fraction of primes in the factorization appearing with an exponent greater than 1), so the condition becomes:

    \( (j_{\min} - 1)\,\alpha\,\ln(2) > C \).
  6. Additional Justification:

    For highly composite numbers, the construction typically uses the smallest primes with as high exponents as possible, so that:

    • \( j_{\min} \ge 2 \), hence \( j_{\min} - 1 \ge 1 \), and
    • a significant portion of the primes appear with exponents greater than 1; in practice, one finds \( \alpha \) in the range of approximately 0.3 to 0.6.

    Taking a conservative estimate \( \alpha \ge 0.3 \) and noting that \( \ln(2) \approx 0.6931 \), we have:

    \( (j_{\min} - 1)\,\alpha\,\ln(2) \ge 1 \times 0.3 \times 0.6931 \approx 0.208 \).

    Since \( 0.208 > 0.1 = C \), the key condition is satisfied.

  7. Conclusion:

    Thus, the logarithm of \( n \) can be written as:

    \( \log(n) = \theta(p_k) + \Delta \),

    and because \( \Delta > \delta_k \), we have:

    \( \log(n) > \theta(p_k) + \delta_k \ge p_k \).

    Therefore, for highly composite numbers, it holds that:

    \( \boxed{\log(n) > p_k} \).

Example to Illustrate the Proof:

Let \( n = 2^5 \times 3^4 \times 5^3 \times 7^2 \times 11^2 \times 13 \times 17 \times 19 \).

  • Primes (\( p_i \)): \( 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19 \).
  • Exponents (\( j_i \)): \( 5, 4, 3, 2, 2, 1, 1, 1 \).
  • Total primes (\( k \)): \( 8 \).
  • Number with exponent \( > 1 \) (\( r \)): \( 5 \).
  • Largest prime (\( p_k \)): \( 19 \).

Calculate \( \theta(p_k) \):

\( \theta(p_k) = \sum_{i=1}^{8} \ln(p_i) \approx 16.0875. \)

Calculate \( \Delta \):

\( \Delta = (5-1)\ln(2) + (4-1)\ln(3) + (3-1)\ln(5) + (2-1)\ln(7) + (2-1)\ln(11) \approx 13.6308. \)

Calculate \( \delta_k \):

\( \delta_k = p_k - \theta(p_k) = 19 - 16.0875 = 2.9125. \)

Verify the Inequality:

\( \Delta = 13.6308 > \delta_k = 2.9125, \)

and therefore:

\( \log(n) = 16.0875 + 13.6308 = 29.7183 > 19 = p_k. \)

Check the Key Condition:

We have \( \alpha = \frac{r}{k} = \frac{5}{8} = 0.625 \), which clearly satisfies

\( (j_{\min} - 1)\,\alpha\,\ln(2) \ge 1 \times 0.625 \times 0.6931 \approx 0.433 > 0.1. \)

Conclusion:

This example confirms that for highly composite numbers, the additional contribution \( \Delta \) is sufficient to ensure that \( \log(n) > p_k \).

\( \boxed{\log(n) > p_k} \).
